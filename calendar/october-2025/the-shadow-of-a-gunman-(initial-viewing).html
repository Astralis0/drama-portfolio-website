Synopsis

The Shadow of a Gunman by Sean O'Casey is a drama set during the Irish War of Independence, we follow Donal Davoren, he's a poet but everyone that knows him seems to think he's an IRA "Gunman", but he is not. He stays with Séamus Shields in a room in tenement housing, in the same building as several other characters that appear within the play. One of said characters, Mr Maguire, leaves a bag in the room, at the start of the play, like a Chekov's Gun[1] I suppose, but instead of a gun that has to be fired by the third act, it's a bag that has to be opened by the end.
When Donal sees that everyone actually thinks he's a "Gunman" he plays into it, enjoying the attention and the air of mystery that it lends him, he calls himself "The Shadow of a Gunman" in a serious voice when he's alone in a scene. Another character, Minnie Powell, is the love interest of Donal, and falls in love with Donal, partially over him being a "Gunman" despite him not being one.
Eventually this act of being a "Gunman" comes back to bite him for two reasons, a tenant from a neighbouring tenement, James Gallogher, comes to him with Julia Henderson asking for the IRA's help, and he gives Donal a letter. Another tenant of the tenement is Tommy Owens, a character that had showed up praising Donal and his involvement with the IRA. He sings out the window about the IRA and the "RA" which probably had some weight in the "Black and Tans" conducting a search of the tenement housing.
And this is where our little Chekov's Bag comes to fruition, Donal and Séamus, check around the room to see if there's anything incriminating, the pair check the bag and realise it's full of bombs, Minnie takes them and hides them in her room because they won't search her room. Donal also remembers the letter which he frantically searches so he can burn it and get rid of it. Eventually the "Black and Tans" arrive and search the place, making a mess. After searching the building they arrest Minnie and take her away, but their wagon is ambushed by the IRA and she's shot.

My Thoughts

Personally I really enjoyed the drama, it's very entertaining and surprisingly tense, a lot of the humour still lands despite it being written a century ago. I really liked the use of lighting with the spotlights which were used sparingly, but when they were used they were very impactful, as well as with the Sun and Moon as physical objects that could be seen on the stage, it really helped with the immersion. The set design overall was fantastic, it looked lived in and real, rather than some artificial location, which is only made possible because of the singular location that is used throughout the drama. I also loved the way the wall split to show the stairway when the characters were looking out into it, that was very clever. The accents and facial expressions were very on point, they were extremely well done and put together. We only saw a recorded version of the show but I wouldn't be opposed to seeing a live performance.
